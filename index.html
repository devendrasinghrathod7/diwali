About Diwali

Diwali, also known as Deepavali, is one of the most widely celebrated festivals in India and among the Indian diaspora worldwide. It is known as the "Festival of Lights" and symbolizes the triumph of light over darkness and good over evil.

Significance of Diwali

Diwali marks the return of Lord Rama to his kingdom, Ayodhya, after 14 years of exile. To celebrate his victory over the demon king Ravana and his return to the kingdom, the people of Ayodhya lit rows of oil lamps (diyas) to welcome him.

Celebration and Traditions

During Diwali, people decorate their homes with diyas, rangoli (colorful floor patterns), and candles. Fireworks are a key part of the celebration, along with family gatherings, prayers, and feasts. The festival also involves the exchange of gifts, sweets, and greetings. It is a time for renewal, bringing prosperity, and strengthening bonds with loved ones.

Cultural Importance

Diwali is celebrated by millions of people across different cultures, and its traditions may vary depending on region and community. While its religious significance is prominent in Hinduism, the festival is also celebrated by Sikhs, Jains, and Buddhists in various forms.
